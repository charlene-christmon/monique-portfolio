Based In

Atlanta, GA — open to senior marketing leadership roles in health, wellness, consumer, and behavior-change organizations.

My career has never stayed in one lane. It has moved through retail operations, brand management, global innovation, coaching, and marketing leadership—but the central question running through all of it has stayed exactly the same: what motivates people, and how do we help them take action?

I started in retail with Marshall Field’s and Macy’s, managing teams, analyzing sales data, and learning early that strategies succeed or fail at the point of execution. That grounding in commercial reality stayed with me.

After my MBA at UNC Kenan-Flagler, I joined Johnson & Johnson and helped lead the relaunch of the TYLENOL® Cold portfolio following product recalls. Operating in a highly regulated environment taught me that marketing requires more than creative thinking—it requires operational discipline, stakeholder alignment, and the ability to move cross-functional teams through complexity.

“Growth follows clarity. Clarity about the customer, the opportunity, and the problem worth solving.”

At Campbell Soup Company, I led the $29M launch of a new Goldfish product and later drove 8.8% growth in the Pepperidge Farm cookies portfolio in a flat category—finding room to grow where others saw stagnation. At InterContinental Hotels Group, I used conjoint analysis and global customer research to identify the moments that most shaped the Holiday Inn guest experience, then led that insight all the way through prototype testing and global rollout.

Along the way, I became a certified health coach and launched a coaching practice. It deepened my understanding of motivation, behavior change, and the factors that influence decision-making. That perspective continues to shape how I approach marketing, leadership, and growth.

Today at Emory’s Goizueta Business School, I lead integrated marketing strategy across MBA and Executive Education—overseeing an $8M budget, driving 16K+ leads, and contributing to +14% YoY revenue growth. The industries have changed, but the work has stayed consistent: understand what drives people, clarify the value an organization offers, and build marketing that earns attention, trust, and action.

Reframing the growth challenge behind a +31% revenue lift.

Executive Education Growth Transformation — Emory Goizueta

The Challenge

Executive Education was underperforming relative to its program quality and market position. Lead volume was low and conversion was inconsistent.

The Insight

The growth challenge was fundamentally a positioning and messaging problem. The programs were not being communicated in the language of the people who needed them most.

The Strategy

Led a rigorous messaging and segmentation overhaul. Introduced A/B testing discipline across email and digital channels. Tightened agency relationships and sharpened channel strategy based on data.

+31% revenue growth. +200% lead volume. An insight-first approach to what looked like a distribution problem.

How I Think

Perspective

Growth challenges are often positioning challenges.

Before assuming the product needs to change or the budget needs to grow, I ask: does the right customer understand why this is for them? In my experience, the clearest path to growth is usually clearer communication, not more spend.

Not all customer moments matter equally.

Conjoint analysis at IHG taught me that a small number of touchpoints drive a disproportionate share of perception and loyalty. Great marketing strategy is partly about knowing which moments to invest in—and which to let go.

Marketing is behavior change.

Every campaign is ultimately asking someone to do something different—to try a product, to renew a subscription, to schedule an appointment. Understanding the psychology of that ask is what separates effective marketing from noise.

Strong brands are built through clarity, not noise.

The brands I admire know who they are, say it simply, and say it consistently. Distinctiveness beats reach when resources are limited. The hard work is figuring out the one thing that is true and actually matters to your customer.

The best strategies begin with better questions.

What is the actual problem we are trying to solve? Who exactly are we talking to, and what do they need to believe to take action? I have found that slowing down at the beginning to ask harder questions saves enormous time and money later.
